METHODOLOGY NOTE

This document serves as a comprehensive component selection guide rather than a fixed blueprint for a single ROV configuration. I recognize that every DIY builder has unique budget constraints, mission requirements, and fabrication capabilities. This BoD systematically evaluates the pros, cons, trade-offs, and implementation considerations for many major subsystems, including hull materials, end-cap sealing methods, sensor payloads, thrusters, and tether management. Although it includes my rationale for Project SOSORUM’s specific design choice, it is not always the best answer for each project.

The goal of Project SOSORUM is to empower you to make informed decisions and help the future of underwater exploration. You will not find “the correct answer.” Instead, you will find the engineering rationale needed to mix and match components to build a submersible that fits your specific needs, whether that’s infrastructure inspection, a deep-sea vehicle, or a micro ROV.

I wrote this document informally rather than making myself sound smart to make it accessible to the common person.

PROJECT GOALS

01

GOAL 1 – Making ROVs Accessible:

Earth’s waters are left unexplored, while ROVs are gatekept behind expensive parts and difficult-to-find information. Building a high-quality, lower-cost ROV and publishing documents that clearly show DIY ROV design trade-offs can open the waters to greater research, surveying, protection, mapping, and search and rescue.

“Affordable” solutions of today that match my ROV cost > $5000, such as Blue Robotics’ BlueROV2.

Making a low-cost ROV not only gives normal enthusiasts the freedom to view the underwater world but also enables larger fleets to be deployed in a wider variety of situations without costing extra money, supporting search-and-recovery and surveying efforts.

Making ROVs accessible also means making this guiding document readable. Not slinging technical terms and speaking ultra-formally, but instead like a normal, understandable human.

02

GOAL 2 – Help Austin’s Barton Creek Salamander, Zebra Mussel, and Toxic Algae Bloom Crises:

I’ve lived in Austin all my life, and we are facing so many extreme water crises. I paddleboard above water that my dogs and I can’t swim in because of toxic algae blooms. I end up boiling water for days because of bacteria infiltrating water supplies. I swim in the same pool where a salamander species is exclusive to and is slowly going extinct underneath my freestyle strokes. I cut my feet on Zebra Mussels while they clog important infrastructure.

Austin is filled with water crises, and I want to make a solution. Where ecological surveys can be easily conducted, and the spread of bacteria and algae, no matter the depth, can be studied, while endangered species can be surveyed and restored, while invasive species can be monitored and prevented.

03

GOAL 3 – Pioneer Multi-ROV and Micro-ROV Systems And Explore Their Applications To Search And Recovery And Infrastructure Inspection:

I’ve loved micro submarines and the possibilities they can open for monitoring pipes and surveying cramped spaces, but they are fundamentally problematic: low power capacity, low electronic capabilities, and slow speeds. That is why I wanted to pioneer a new idea: combining a macro and micro submarine to eliminate the impracticalities of micro subs while gaining their specific benefits. Using a micro sub docked on a large sub, problems related to electronic capabilities, power, and going large distances between survey locations are solved.

Micro subs also present benefits in search and recovery, where rubble may block traditional, boxy big subs like the BlueRobotics Sub, a micro sub can still investigate. The main barrier to the feasibility of this is that SAR often needs to happen quickly, and ROVs need to investigate for long periods, ultimately eliminating micro-subs, but demonstrating how my macro-micro sub system can be effective. Although I want to cater this sub to ecological efforts rather than SAR, I hope I can use it as a demonstration for a SAR-catered project.

Tether:

Options:

  • Blue Robotics Tether (CAT 6): This was the original plan: BlueRobotics ROV tether, but the cost would be $450+ dollars, while power would struggle due to it being CAT6-sized (1A max). For freshwater, which is less corrosive and goes to smaller depths (less pressure-rated needed), normal CAT6 cables should work. The massive positive is that it is neutrally buoyant as well as waterproof; its neutral buoyancy is made for seawater, which may mean it becomes positively buoyant in our freshwater application.
  • LLDPE CAT 6: Although immediately appealing because of its higher waterproofing capabilities that allow for greater depths, its inflexibility is problematic for the maneuverability of the submarine and for surface operations like spooling. However, although restraining, it does work for underwater applications. Another issue is that it holds its shape relatively well. To test LLDPE’s viability, I sourced some from a scrap satellite. Here are some images that demonstrate the stiffness. LLDPE cantilevering 2+ft over the workbench without any support, only its own stiffness. LLDPE holds random twists and spirals in it instead of forming a catenary (u-shape) mid-air.
  • Silicone CAT 6: The most appealing option because it has higher waterproofing, like LLDPE CAT6, while maintaining flexibility, allowing for both maneuverability for the submarine and spooling above the surface. However, it faces a similar problem to the Blue Robotics tether of cost and availability. It is also prone to kinking and damage, ultimately making it a mediocre option.
  • PVC (with HDPE) CAT 6: This is the most abundant but slightly concerning option. PVC jackets are semi-permeable, which means PVC cables cannot be submerged. This only becomes a problem for long-term, multi-day periods of submersion, where water can work its way through micropores. Since it is the most abundant, it is affordable and available, making it appealing. Furthermore, the inner wires of common “PVC” wires are jacketed with fully waterproof HDPE, HDPE having a tight cellular structure. If the HDPE jacket has no flaws, it will work to deep depths. The main thing to think about is that even if the HDPE inner jacket protects the conductors, water will make it through the PVC jacket and then down the tether toward your penetrator. Thus, you need to seal the wire ends properly and use a high-quality penetrator like Wet Link. It is ultimately the option I went with because it saves me $100 compared to a PUR jacketed cable, and full LLDPE is too inflexible.
  • TPE CAT 6: It has similar properties to LLDPE but is flexible, allowing it to be waterproof, pressure-rated, durable, and maneuverable, perfect for an ROV. It is a competitive choice, but its rarity ultimately meant that for > 100-foot sections, prices became absurd rather than staying around $1/foot. For price and a lack of availability, I didn’t choose it.
  • PUR CAT 6: This was the jacket I landed on. Although expensive (~$0.6/foot), it has unmatched durability, flexibility, and waterproofing, ultimately allowing it to go to deeper depths. Furthermore, it is available since it has common applications in robotics.

Jacket Style:

  • Round: This is common and offers equal drag and flexibility in all directions. It is a good option if you are using a jacket made out of a flexible material. Most penetrators are made for round cables, making it a good option.
  • Flat: This is a less common option but still widely available, allowing greater flexibility with stiffer materials like LLDPE. It is nonuniformly bendable and experiences varying drag depending on its orientation in the water. The main problem with it is that standard penetrators like the WaterLink Penetrator by Blue Robotics only work for round cables.

Pressure-Induced Signal Degradation:

Another benefit of PUR in comparison to materials like silicone is their anti-kinking material prevents signal loss; however, signal loss is not only caused by kinking but also by high pressure. For this reason, filling the CAT6 cable with oil or gel is ideal to prevent the jacket from collapsing. Doing this DIY is tricky, and off-the-shelf, it becomes nearly as expensive as BlueRobotics’ tether. Pressure-induced signal degradation on a stock CAT6 cable is likely to occur around 45 meters. For freshwater applications, this submarine will be used for depths that will not reach past 30 meters.

Tether Buoyancy:

One of the biggest positives of the BlueRobotics tether is that it is already neutrally buoyant (in saltwater), while any common CAT6 cable is negatively buoyant. To be able to use regular CAT6, flotation devices such as pressure-rated foam can be used. These flotation devices must be pressure-rated; they can collapse underwater or release air bubbles, ultimately causing them to sink. There are two main negatives with discrete flotation devices: the extra size catching on underwater plants, and on the surface, spooling being very messy.

For my purposes, I will be using closed-cell rubber nitrile pipe insulation, buying 12 ft and cutting it into 1-inch segments (144 pieces placed). How often I place them will depend on the density of the cable. The easiest way for me to do this is through physical calculations and confirming with tests underwater. During testing, make sure not to compromise your cable by letting the unsealed ends get wet.

Wire Core and Water Wicking:

  • Copper Cladded Aluminum: Cheaper, works fine for low amperage/speed applications. For an ROV, CCA will not work for PoE applications as it causes a high voltage drop and cannot carry as much amperage due to its high resistance. See the Power section for a spreadsheet comparing the voltage drop of CCA vs Cu.
  • Copper: Although more expensive, copper is necessary for PoE applications as its lower resistance decreases voltage drop and allows for high amperage without overheating.
  • Solid: For every cable, there are two inner core options: Solid and Braided. Solid core is cheaper and prevents water from wicking through the cable like a straw; it is extremely inflexible, ultimately making it worse for a maneuverable submarine.
  • Braided: Braided allows for water wicking, which means if the inner core is nicked or the exposed ends touch moisture, water will travel down the wire, into the submarine’s housing, and ultimately fry electronics. To prevent this, a double-insulated cable (like CAT6) is beneficial, so it takes an extreme event to cause problematic damage. Furthermore, end sealing must be considered in capsule penetration.

Capsule Penetration:

  • WetLink Penetrator: This is a slightly expensive but worthwhile investment to pass cables from the wet to the dry environment. It has a simple, organized, and well-documented process that leaves your cables neat. It also has a superior pressure rating. The only downside is cost ($13-17), adaptability (only some cables work), and availability (mainly on Blue Robotics).
  • Potted Penetrators: This is a cheaper penetrator (~$5) that allows cables to be passed into the hull. It does not have as much strength and waterproofing as a Blue Robotics WetLink Penetrator. Its upside is simplicity, cost, and availability. It requires epoxy for waterproofing the wires, so chosen cable insulation must bond well. The penetrator provides structural support to the end cap preventing implosion, unlike direct epoxy.
  • Direct Epoxy: By fanning out each conductor into its separate wires and applying epoxy, you can achieve a watertight seal. It is extremely cheap but is difficult to do high quality, can lead to shorts, can’t go to higher depths, and the cable’s material must bond well with epoxy. Make sure to sand down the jacket for a good grip. It can work well in applications with small tethers and small endcaps (such as my micro subs) because normal penetrators don’t work for small tethers, and the smaller the endcap the less risk of implosion due to using this technique. PUR specifically struggles to bond to epoxy, especially after many bend cycles, and its difficulty is multiplied when the technique is used with a CAT6 cable, since you must fan out all the conductors neatly without them touching. For this reason, I am not using this for the tether, but it is a valid option for the motor wires. Below is an image showing the proper technique before applying epoxy.

Power:

Power Budgeting:

To have a powerful motor on the main submarine and multiple small motors on the small sub for high maneuverability alongside immense data collection and secondary control systems (like ballast motors and camera servos) means the amperage budget needs to be surprisingly high.

Power Systems:

There are 3 methods of powering the submarine, each of which I pivoted to chronologically:

PoE:

Power over the CAT6 (known as PoE) allows the submarine to run infinitely and have only 1 small cord, but the problem is that CAT6 can only handle ~.96 amp per pair of wires, and with at least 4 used up by control, and (possibly) data on another pair, that leaves .9-1.8 amps of power possible. This ultimately makes it impossible to power any sub that can move moderately fast.

Cable Bundling:

Although PoE has strict amperage limits, using lower gauge wire can increase your amperage limit, such that you can send enough power over for your sub. The problem is that lower gauge wire alongside CAT6 communication wire starts to become extremely expensive, especially pure copper, making it a bad solution for my low budget. Furthermore, any above-water power system is going to have the problem of resistance, causing a voltage drop. With 200+ feet of cable, resistance (R=⍴*L/A) is high, meaning you must use a higher voltage supply with an onboard regulator inside the sub. This means batteries start to become expensive and annoying to use, and ultimately an inefficient solution. The last reason and probably the most problematic part of a cable bundle is that it would create an extremely thick tether, reducing maneuverability and weighing down the sub while making surface tether management extremely difficult. Battery:

Batteries work great except that they are environmentally hazardous and, of course, run out of charge after a certain amount of time. Here are the options for batteries:

  • NiMH: Much safer for the environment than lithium batteries and more stable; however, they have low energy density and extremely low discharge rating, making them unsuitable for an ROV powering strong motors (especially brushless).
  • NiCd: A similarly bad energy density and discharge rating to NiMH, but without the environmental positives, as Cadmium is toxic.
  • Li-ion: Good energy density and discharge rating, but they carry a similar environmental hazard to LiPo without the added energy density. For a submersible where weight and size matter, it isn’t the best choice.
  • LiPo: Great with high discharge ratings and energy density, with the one downside of being less stable and environmentally hazardous.

Despite some of these batteries being safer for the environment than others, none of them are safe. Per my constraint of environmental friendliness, building a sub with a battery means I MUST integrate an emergency recovery system. See section: Emergency Recovery Systems.

Trickle-Charging PoE

The biggest problem, besides the environmental friendliness of batteries, is that they run out of charge. This can lead to fatal accidents where batteries are mismanaged, and the ROV runs out of batteries before making it back to the station. The best way to solve this is something I’ve called trickle-charging.

Although PoE cannot provide enough power to make a high-speed ROV and maintain an ROV's position at higher currents, it can still produce a considerable output. By dedicating 1-2 pairs of the CAT6 to power while having an onboard battery, the tether can help extend the running time of the ROV, and if the battery level is mismanaged, it can slowly recharge/move the ROV so it can get to the surface.

The biggest concern creators should consider for trickle-charging PoE is that it adds electronic complexity, dangerous heat buildup, and inefficient cost. For my ROV that needs to be cost-effective, adding a complex charging circuit, heat dissipation systems, and a big surface battery is not worth the small benefits it gives.

Battery Capacity:

Choosing a battery capacity can be difficult because it is a trade-off between the length of your runs and speed. Although weight does not impact top speed as much in ROVs, since the limiter of top speed is drag underwater, it does affect the time it takes to accelerate. The bigger the battery, the more sluggish your ROV will feel. Many DIY ROVs use a battery pack around 5000mAh to 20000mAh. To give a reference, the BlueROV2 uses an 18000mAh battery and has a battery life of ~2 hours with their thrusters. One way to figure out what size you need is to work backward from your power budget calculations to estimated run time. Also think about your ROV’s function. Is it to conduct slow, long-time environmental surveys, or is it to make a fast ROV (lower weight is more important than runtime)? It's also dependent on size since a smaller ROV requires smaller thrusters that draw less amperage (and thus need less battery capacity for the same runtime).

Multiple Batteries:

Using two 8000mAh battery packs in my design saved over $40. Battery prices often grow a lot faster than the extra capacity, making running multiple in parallel (same voltage, adding capacity) an economical choice. You will need to buy a parallel balance board.

Battery Voltage:

If you are doing a PoE system, you will need to buy a higher voltage battery, probably at least 48 volts, but you should complete calculations to figure out the required voltage vs amperage output (See PoE section spreadsheet). To figure out the voltage required for an onboard battery / the ROV input side of PoE, you should look at your brushless motors and figure out what RPM you want to achieve for your chosen propellers. For a 350kV motor (like mine) with 70mm props, online sources say I should aim for a 3000-4000 rpm, which means a 11.1V 3S Lipo is perfect because 11.1V*350kV=3885rpm

Battery C-Rating:

The C rating of a battery is how many amps it can output. A 1000 mAh NiMH battery with a 1C discharge rating, for example, can output 1 amp continuously. To figure out what C-rating you need, take the Amp hours of your batteries and figure out what number, C, you need to multiply it by to get the max amp pull of your ROV. For example: a 3Ah (3000mAh) 3S Lipo that needs to power a max of 60 Amps would need (at least) a 20C discharge rating because 3 [Ah] * [minimum] C = 60 [Amps]. Most Lipos will be fine, because C rating only really becomes an issue if you had crazy amperages or super small lipos. For other batteries like NiMH, C ratings will be extremely low (often unlisted, but 1-5C), making them difficult to use.

Battery Bay:

If you are making an open-source or multi-purpose ROV, the battery bay is a more important thing than for the average DIY ROV. Consider making the battery bay have interchangeable sleeves such that it can accommodate slightly different battery sizes, allowing for people to use different brands and slightly different capacities. For example, my ROV will have a sleeve for 2x 8000mAh batteries, but if people want to – for example – upgrade to two 9000 or 10000 mAh batteries, or one big 20000 mAh battery, exchanging the sleeve for their own custom sleeve will allow the batteries to fit properly. Another design consideration in the battery bay is thermal dissipation, especially if you choose trickle charging PoE or are making a racing ROV. One may consider putting heat sinks in their battery bay, leaving spots open to the hull’s air, fans, or thermometers.

Thruster Array:

Although the transit mode has few requirements for thrusters, precise station hold requires full directional authority. This is advantageous as it makes the ROV easier to drive and more maneuverable. Achieving this means a 6 thruster array, where the middle’s sides near the center of buoyancy have vertical thrusters allowing for fine vertical control and faster emergency surfacing. The other 4 thrusters are arranged on the edges of the frame, pointed 45 degrees inward, such that they can be used for both forward/back and left/right as well as yaw.

Pitch Authority:

This setup does not give pitch authority. To have pitch authority, you need an 8 thruster setup, which is costly, and pitch does not give many advantages. Passively, the pitch is managed by weight balance during design. If properly done, the ROV should stay practically level without active control. The one advantage pitch control gives is a better viewing angle; this can also be achieved by placing a servo on the camera instead. This is what I plan to do since it gives more options for optical flow station holding and is generally very nice to use. Another option to achieve pitch control is having a heavy component on a servo or leadscrew. For example, by sliding a battery forward/backward in the hull, you can change the center of buoyancy to tilt the ROV.

One big advantage of this thruster array is that the direct front/back are not occluded by thrusters, allowing for the camera to see well and the tether to not clip any propellers.

Thruster Motors:

Brushed

These are motors that use a brush on the inside, which wears out over time, leading to a short lifespan or maintenance; however, they have lower upfront costs, and they are simpler to control, just using voltage changes. They can be fully submerged in water, but the bearing isn’t waterproof and will seize, so it is recommended to be replaced. For my-sized ROV, I would use RS 540/550 motors; RS 380/390 are also common.

BrushlessChosen

These are motors that do not use any physical contact inside, so they have a longer lifespan and lower maintenance. They are more power efficient than brushed but draw high amperages. KV denotes how many rotations per minute happen per volt; ROV brushless motors should generally be less than 700 kV and most often are 300-500 kV. They have higher upfront costs and require a more complex ESC to control them. These can be fully submerged in water, but the bearing also isn’t waterproof and will seize, so you need to replace it. It is also recommended to treat the wires with epoxy to extend their lifetime.

Motor Location & Power Transmission:

Magnetically Coupled Thruster

Using magnets, arranging them in opposite polarity in a disc, you can transmit power through a dry area to the wet propeller. This is often used for “transit function thruster arrays,” where the ROV has one back thruster. It allows your motor to stay dry, prolonging its life and meaning you don’t have to worry about wire penetrators, replacing bearings, water wicking down wires, etc. Its main downside is that although it can be simple to make a basic version, making an efficient one is difficult. Many basic designs fail to have good efficiency and ultimately end up weak. Here is a working design created by MrMikefrye on YouTube: https://youtu.be/zdjgxerAekQ

WetChosen

This is common for brushless motors, where you place the motor directly into the water, allowing water to fill it. Motors are waterproof (besides the bearing), so it works well. The little friction and no extra parts mean it is an efficient and cheap option. You may have to seal the motor’s contacts appropriately and make sure that water doesn’t wick down the cord.

Propeller Housing:

The propellers should have a proper housing to prevent thruster entanglement (the propeller getting clogged with sea plants). It should have a shroud (A cylindrical shell that goes on the outside of the propeller) and a fan guard (A cover of mesh or metal wire) on the front/back faces.

Kort-Style Nozzle

Kort-style nozzles are hydrodynamically designed shrouds that are optimized to increase thrust of marine propellers by creating a pressure differential with an airplane-wing-like cross section. However, they are only optimized in one direction so they are not great for bidirectional thrusters on ROVs.

Cylindrical ShroudsChosen

As opposed to kort-style nozzles cylindrical shrouds are not optimized for maximum thrust but they work the best bidirectionally.

Propellers:

  • Bought Propellers: High quality, strong, but can be difficult to find the right type for your motor; most aren’t made for ROVs, and those that are made for ROVs are expensive. Good choice if you have the extra money.
  • 3D Printed Propellers: 3D printing propellers allow you to save cost while also optimizing the design for your specific setup. You must design it carefully, see Designing a Propeller below. If you are 3D printing your propeller you need to make sure it is strong enough as high RPMs can rip it apart. You also need to make it smooth, as this massively increases efficiency. I recommend achieving these requirements by printing in ABS (strong), 100% infill (so it doesn't fill with water), and then vapor smoothing it with acetone. See section: Vapor Smoothing under Construction.
  • Uni- vs Bi-directional Propellers: ROV propellers should be different from drone propellers. They should have a smaller pitch since water is viscous and thus harder to push. Most ROVs benefit from bidirectional propellers. Drone propellers are specifically designed to be efficient in one direction, but choosing this on an ROV means that differential thrust can feel sluggish, as relying on just one motor to create rotation takes longer underwater, while running the differential pair in opposite directions makes it feel responsive.
  • Designing Efficient Propellers: Some ways to design an efficient propeller are visually copying an already used design, using design tools like OpenProp, and testing your propeller with a rig. Some researchers have found 3D-printed props to work well underwater; use OpenThruster, so you can use that resource alongside BlueRobotics, Homebuilt ROVs, and r/ROV Forums.
  • Pitch: Use calculators like: https://boatcalculators.com.au/propeller-efficiency/, to optimize the pitch of your given propeller.

ESC / Motor Controllers:

  • Brushed Motor Controllers: For brushed motors, h-bridge bidirectional motor controllers are cheap and common, one reason brushed motors can be a better economic choice. They work by changing the voltage output to your motor, which changes the speed.
  • Brushless ESCs: For brushless motors, you need an ESC. These are more expensive, and searching specifically for a bidirectional one can be tricky. Sometimes results will not be listed as bidirectional, but the software they have supports bidirectionality. They work by changing the generated three-phase pulse. Since ROVs almost always have 4+ motors, it is often a simple, more economic choice to buy multi-motor ESC boards, the most available versions being 4 motors in 1 ESC.

Redundancy:

ESCs can have mixed quality, especially if you are trying to save cost. Since it is an essential component for ROV control, it is important to integrate redundancy. There are two ways to do this without adding extra cost: having one ESC per motor, and splitting multi-motor ESCs in a smart method. I recommend splitting multi-motor ESCs such that if one fails, you still have enough control to recover the ROV; for example, my ROV has the front two motors and a vertical thruster linked on one ESC and the back two motors and a vertical thruster linked on another. Thus, if one ESC fails, I still have forward/back control, yaw control, and some up/down control, allowing me to recover the ROV.

Video System:

Analog vs Digital:

Digital systems are more expensive and use tons of cables rather than just one signal cable like analog, their cables often being MIPI cables. This means that your tether would need to include tons of micro conductors, which is impossible to buy and the interference across 200+ feet would ruin the image. For this reason, analog is the only choice.

Servo Controlled Camera:

A cheap $3 upgrade (one servo) that can make your view so much better. By adding a servo to the bottom of your camera you can use buttons on your controller to look left and right. It costs practically nothing and is a very nice feature. You can add a second servo to have full view control, but then it starts to be difficult to control without head tracking (which is difficult).

Interference and Baluns:

Running 200+ feet of analog video is a recipe for a static nightmare from interference with other data running in the CAT6. This is where baluns come in. Just running the signal cable pair is Signal (data) and GND (0), and interference will only affect the signal line so it becomes: Signal (data + random interference volts) and GND (0). A balun is a transformer that changes the pair to be opposites of each other. So the bottom balun before the tether makes the video conductor pair be: Signal Positive (data + random interference) Signal Negative (opposite of data + random interference) and then the topside balun can improve quality by looking at the difference between the positive and negative signal cables that were hit equally, and subtracting out the interference to get a clean image.

Multi-Sub Video Switching:

Having a multi-sub system is difficult for video because you need to be able to switch between the cameras after deployment. To do this you need to buy a camera switching board that connects to the camera’s analog signals and a PWM of your microcontroller that receives remote control input.

Buoyancy:

Achieving Neutral Buoyancy:

Your ROV needs to be nearly neutral buoyant to work, stay stable, and save battery life. Making it a smidge positively buoyant means that even with a communication loss or battery mismanagement, your ROV will return to the surface for recovery. If your ROV floats or sinks a ton, then you will have to expend lots of energy using vertical thrusters, or your ballast system may not be able to compensate, and you’ll end up on the surface or sea floor. To achieve neutral buoyancy, you must incorporate things less dense in air and make sure they do not collapse under pressure. Here are some options:

  • Closed Cell Foam: Foam where each bubble is separate from one another, making it moisture-resistant. Unlike PVC. It will not experience catastrophic failures, but can slowly absorb water over long dives. To solve the small absorption problem, you can wrap it in waterproof tape. It is also not expensive, and can be found in products such as pool noodles. The foam can compress at deeper depths, ultimately reducing its buoyancy and meaning thrusters/ballasts must compensate more.
  • Hollow Pipes: Most DIYers use PVC pipe as it is sufficiently pressure-resistant, especially schedule 80, and easy to create. It also allows DIYers to fine-tune their buoyancy perfectly by cutting / rearranging pipes until it's perfect. Its biggest con is that it can have a catastrophic failure: an end seal can fail, it can implode, and both will lead to the ROV wanting to sink. Although PVC has similar pressure capabilities to closed cell foam, buying specific thick pipe and calculated pressure ratings using Under Pressure can create a deeper depth float.
  • Syntactic Foam: A composite of glass/plastic micro balloons suspended in epoxy, making it incompressible and rated for deeper depths. It is expensive and not a great option for DIYers.

Ballasts vs Vertical Thrusters:

Although ballasts are used on real-life large-scale submarines, they are not particularly efficient for ROVs. Active ballasts that take in water to change their buoyancy now have to lug that water around for the entire time they’re at that depth. That makes ballast submarines more sluggish. Active ballasts also need to use a tricky predictive algorithm to get to the right depth, which is complex and hard to make easy to work with. Since active ballasts fight water pressure, for example, a 100mL syringe piston ballast at 100 feet needs to resist 62 lbs / 28.12 kg of force trying to push the syringe open. That ultimately means they rely on high-torque motors, lead screws, and worm gears; all slow mechanisms, while a pilot is trying to preemptively change the acceleration by buoyancy to get a desired depth. Another issue with piston ballasts (often the most viable ballast) it is tricky to make pressure-rated. It relies on syringes whose plunger and walls can’t handle deep pressures. This limits the ROV’s depths, and replacing it with a more pressure-resistant piston system becomes costly and difficult, especially as deeper depths require the leadscrew/gear rack system to have a lot more torque (bigger motor, stronger parts). At just 100m (328ft), a 100mL syringe active ballast system faces 203lbs / 92.08 kg of force. The only benefits of active ballast systems are that the mechanisms are epic and they can have slight power savings over vertical thrusters that constantly run. Ultimately, this is why most ROVs rely on vertical thrusters. The small potential power benefits are destroyed by complexity, difficulty to pilot, and bad pressure resistance.

Dry Hull:

Dry Hull Walls:

The dry hull is where all components that must stay dry will go. This will include most electronics. I will be using a 4-inch acrylic tube with .25 inch walls to be able to store a large battery and not run out of space. The main tube will be 12 inches long. The other options for material are:

  • Aluminum: The best for ultra-high depth operations. It has superior pressure resistance and durability. Its main drawbacks are cost, availability, and an inability to easily inspect for water ingress.
  • Acrylic: A very cheap and available option for tubing. Its main drawback is that it isn’t as pressure-resistant as aluminum, so if you plan on going past 100 meters, it should not be used. A big positive it has compared to aluminum is that you can see if undetected water ingress happened during your dive. It can also be difficult to machine, being prone to cracking. It is a good plastic for ROVs because it undergoes deformation over time under high pressure rather than implosion, cracking, etc.
  • Polycarbonate: A more expensive clear tubing that allows you to inspect for water ingress while still maintaining high pressure. It has a higher impact rating than acrylic but is often not as optically clear. Its main usage is if your ROV features flat panels or will have an area that will be bumped into rocks. For specific situations, it can actually be cheaper to source than cast acrylic, so don’t fully rule it out if your main concern is cost. For example, my Micro Sub’s main hull had such a small OD that cast acrylic tubing wasn’t easily available.
  • Poly Vinyl Chloride (PVC): A cheap, versatile option. Great for shallow-water ROVs, but shouldn’t be used for any deep diving ROVs. They have a similar problem to aluminum, where you can’t inspect for water ingress visually. Another advantage is that PVC is easy to machine, unlike acrylic.
  • 3D Printed Plastics: Do not try to use 3D printed plastics for your enclosure, as they really struggle with pressure, and their inconsistency can lead to premature failures. Furthermore, the print lines are semi-permeable, meaning they must have epoxy over all surfaces or else they will have water ingress THROUGH the material. For this reason, all 3D prints on the ROV must also be 100% plastic. That means no infill. Otherwise, the air will cause them to implode / they will fill with water, changing buoyancy and sinking your ROV. To give an example, an ABS flat endcap that is ½ inch thick of pure plastic would fail at 139.54 feet depth according to Under Pressure software. With a 3x safety factor, that means my ROV would be rated to dive to only around 46 feet.

End Caps:

Dome:

Often used on the front where the camera lies, a dome allows for optical clarity. And a greater range of vision. When it comes to dome types, there are two good options:

  • CCTV Acrylic Domes: Domes made for CCTV cameras are made of “2-3mm” thick acrylic with great optical clarity. However, this is often not strong cast acrylic, and the thickness often varies, so although it may be 4mm at the start of the dome, it often becomes 1 near the top, ultimately making pressure resistance lower than believed. These are not great for deep water ROVs but can be an excellent choice for anyone going to shallow depths. I originally wanted to use these with an internal structural frame to increase pressure resistance, but this is a bad idea because inner frames need to use gaskets to prevent pressure points that actually cause premature implosion.
  • Pressure-Rated Dome Endcap by Blue Robotics: A great dome endcap made of polycarbonate for greater impact resistance and produced specifically for ROVs (better pressure rating and has optical clarity). The main con is that they are expensive, and the correct system uses a proprietary O-Ring sealing method that only really works if you buy the entire Blue Robotics enclosure system ($400+). I will be going with this option since they are truly pressure-rated (unlike CCTV domes), but I will be designing my own sealing system for engineering practice and cost.
Flat:

Often used on the back end of the ROV, these are a great option for a simple endcap and are a great place to place wire penetrators. However, there aren’t many good pressure-rated options off the shelf:

  • Blue Robotics Flat Endcaps: Coming in many different materials, such as aluminum, acrylic, etc., the Blue Robotics endcaps are great. The main problem (the same as their dome endcap) is that they are expensive and use a proprietary O-Ring sealing method that only really works if you buy the entire Blue Robotics enclosure system ($400+).
  • DIY Sheets: By buying a sheet of material and cutting it to size, you can make an inexpensive end cap. You will have to design your own sealing mechanism. To choose your material and material thickness, think about machinability since you will have to cut it yourself or pay a company like JLCPCB, cost, and use Under Pressure Software to calculate the pressure rating for any given setup (make sure to include a safety factor!)

Dry Hull Electronics:

  • Microcontroller: The Raspberry Pi Pico serves as the main brain for the macro sub, handling sensor readings and communication with the surface. It's cheap, small, and has enough GPIO to handle the sub's needs without much fuss.
  • Breakout Board: The Pico breakout board makes wiring everything to the microcontroller far less painful. It allows wires to be plugged and screwed in rather than soldered.
  • PWM Controller: PWM controllers like the PCA9685 are important for controlling servos and brushless motor ESCs. Although most microcontrollers feature some PWM pins, most ROV setups will quickly surpass the required amount.
  • ESCs: Some ESCs are waterproof so that you can place them outside the dry hull. It can also be achieved by epoxying ESCs into a box; however, this makes them difficult to replace. The multi-motor brushless ESCs I've chosen to use are not waterproof, so I'll place them inside the dry hull. For more info about ESCs, see section: ESCs and Motor Controller within Thrusters.
  • Motor Controller: My CableCram mechanism uses a brushed motor, so it needs a different motor controller than the brushless motors.
  • Water Ingress Detector: Having a small water detector placed inside the hull can preemptively activate emergency recovery systems before water ingress ruins all electronics inside. It should be placed near the most likely place for water ingress. For most ROVs, this would be where cables enter the ROV.
  • Current Sensor: The INA219 keeps tabs on current draw across the system, which is handy both for battery management and for catching a stalled or shorted thruster before it becomes a bigger problem.
  • RC Transceiver: RS-485 transceivers handle communication between the topside controller and the sub over the tether. RS-485 is a solid choice here since it's differential and holds up well over long cable runs, unlike single-ended signaling.
  • Servos: A small 14g servo is used for the camera pan mechanism.
  • Batteries: 3S 11.1V packs provide the main power for the thrusters and electronics. Sized to give a reasonable runtime without making the buoyancy calculations a nightmare.
  • Cameras: RunCam Nano 2s are small, lightweight analog cameras that punch above their size, giving good image quality without eating into the payload budget.
  • Video Switcher: A 3-way switcher lets the pilot flip between multiple camera feeds (forward, down, etc.) without needing separate monitors or extra wiring complexity topside.
  • Balun Pair: Used to convert between the screw-terminal/CAT6 side and BNC video connections, since the tether uses twisted-pair wiring rather than coax for the video signal.

Electronic Tray:

Electronics should be mounted to an easy-to-remove tray. This is often made with steel standoffs, steel rods, or threaded rods as the main structure that goes through the entire hull. Then it has a variety of plates to mount the electronics to and places to manage cables. It should remain completely separate from the hull (besides the end cap) such that it can be removed.

Organizing Electronics On Tray:

It makes your life a lot easier if you organize your electronics logically. You should both think about the flow (ex: if a board needs to connect with the tether before going to the microcontroller, it should be placed between the microcontroller and tether). Most electronic trays have two sides, and the best way to organize the electronics is to separate noisy and sensitive electronics. Place noisy electronics on one side (like ESCs) and sensitive electronics (like video transmission components) on the other.

Choosing Wires:

The ROV needs wires to run to thrusters, servos, and brushed motors as well as internal electronics, and data boards. To minimize cost I will be reusing the same few wires:

  • Good Jacket Materials: Jacket material for the wires you pick are pretty important if you are using potted penetrators, as epoxy must bond to them. They also need to be waterproof, but for regular wires (not the tether) flexibility luckily doesn’t matter. For my application, I will be using PVC outer jacket + HDPE inner jacket since its cheap, waterproof, and epoxy bonds well with it. PUR, PVC, and neoprene all bond well while silicone, PE, PP, etc. struggle.
  • 18 AWG 3 Conductor Cable: This can carry plenty of amps, comes in at the same 6mm diameter as CAT6 cables (making buying and installing potted penetrators efficient). Having 3 conductors means they can work for brushless motors, servos, and brushed motors (by leaving one not connected).
  • CAT6: This cannot carry many amps, but holds 8 conductors which means that one penetrator can receive way more data. This is what I will be using for my data boards and tethers.
  • Dupont Cables: They are cheap and common, they can be used throughout the internal electronics of the sub (when they aren’t carrying many amps). Another choice is to take some CAT6 wire and take the outer jacket off to get small conductors for wiring boards together. I do not recommend using the pin headers on their alone (especially not for extremely important connections) because they can fall out easily and sometimes not make a connection even when plugged in.

Cable Cram – Micro Sub Deployment System:

Locking Mechanism:

The locking system needs to be controllable by electronics, giving us options like electromagnets, servos, and motors. Since the Micro Sub Bay is positioned in the middle of the underside of the Macro Sub, it isn’t easy to have wires or electronics that cross from wet to dry. Every cable that goes from wet to dry requires a $14+ penetrator. That means the best choice is to pass motion through the walls using magnets. Electromagnets consume energy the whole time they’re on, making them inefficient. A servo can hold magnets that keep the micro sub in place until it moves far enough away that the micro sub’s magnet isn’t in range, at which point it disconnects.

Tether Management:

This is the most difficult part of the entire sub because I cannot find any off-the-shelf options… so I am inventing my own design. Here are the documented tether management systems that fail:

  • Spool: The most common tether management system, but they require a slip ring, which is expensive and complex on its own, but a pressure-resistant slip ring… that costs $4000. If it didn’t need a slip ring, the spool would be great for its simplicity.
  • Passive Cable Storage: Coiling a tether in either a figure 8 or helically circumvents the slip ring, and the ROV can pull it out as it drives. It is passive storage, and passively goes out. But it is similar to pulling the yarn out of a ball of yarn; once you pull it out, you can never put it back in, ultimately meaning the MicroSub can only be deployed once, and once it redocks, the tether will just be hanging out. For this reason, passive storage techniques don’t work for my purpose.
  • CableCram: The MicroSub’s tether management system I am working on is called CableCram; rather than trying to establish a uniform state inside, which would require a pulley with an outrageously expensive slip ring or an overcomplicated figure 8 rewinding mechanism, it tolerates the randomness. This means it has an oversized bucket that the tether lies in, and two gripping wheels on the top and bottom of the cable, at least one motorized, can pull the tether in and out, allowing the micro sub to be deployed multiple times. The bucket needs to be oversized to accommodate the random twists and turns, and the wheels need to have a good grip to be able to force the cable inside. Fortunately, this does not add much weight since it doesn’t need to be pressure-resistant, as the tether storage area can be flooded. It also doesn’t necessarily need to add much drag, because it can be 3D printed to be a shape that is efficient for drag and your specific frame requirements. It doesn’t need to hold much cable since the micro ROV is an extension of the other sub, which can move alongside it / dock to it. I plan to include 10-25 feet of ultra slim cable.

Motor:

CableCram is motorized to take out slack and make the micro sub redeployable. The motor RPM needs to be low to match the relatively slow feed in/out as the micro sub travels. Although I have avoided brushed motors so far in the design, for this it makes the most sense. A geared down RS 370 is the most efficient motor for this. To control the RS370 I will be using a Pololu DRV8874.

Position Knowledge:

  • Friction Clutch: By placing a friction clutch between the motor and the output wheel that moves the tether when the end is reached, the friction clutch will slip, stopping any damage to the tether. The main problem is that it doesn’t give any feedback to the microcontroller and small struggles when pulling in/out cable may trigger the clutch (without the microcontroller knowing) causing the entire system to break.
  • Current Sensing: The DRV8833 motor driver I plan to use includes current sensing. Using this feature I can place discs before the tether ends that the system can’t physically pull in. This will stop the motor and cause amperage to spike. The DRV8833 can detect this and knows to automatically stop and mark it as the end/start of the tether using the microcontroller.

Active vs Passive CableCram:

I originally planned to have CableCram actively manage the tether, where it deploys at the same rate as the ROV drives away, however this would require a waterproof encoder (around $1000 for submersible encoders) and a tether tension measurement system (extremely complex to integrate and hard to waterproof). But for a 25ft tether an always-active tether management system does not provide much benefit compared to the cost and complexity. My plan is instead to have a fully in/out system. When you hit deploy, the tether starts coming out at a speed similar to the normal speed of the micro ROV but does not use an active feedback system. When you hit the button again to suck the tether in, the tether sucks in at the motor's maximum speed (since you should be at the bay when you hit the button).

External Electronics:

  • Waterproof Brushless Motors: What powers the propellers for thrust, and can be fully submerged.
  • Heavy-Duty Waterproof Servos: Servos that are meant to be submerged, heavy-duty so they can run the water sampling mechanism despite the friction caused by the rubber bands.
  • Pressure Sensor: Measures water pressure, must be epoxied into a box with only the sensor exposed.
  • Temperature Sensor: Measures water temperature, must be epoxied into a box with only a probe exposed.
  • Turbidity Sensor: Measures water turbidity by shining light, must be epoxied into a box with only the separate sensor exposed.
  • TDS Sensor: Measures total dissolved solids, must be epoxied into a box with only a probe exposed.
  • Dive Lights: I bought separate dive lights because they are much cheaper than waterproofing LED lights myself or buying waterproofed LEDs that are controlled by the microcontroller. Since the lights almost never need to be turned off, being separately powered and uncontrolled isn't an issue, you just click them on before deployment.

Station Holding Systems:

The greatest difficulty with a macro-micro sub is that when the micro sub is deployed, the macro sub needs to stay pretty much in place. To do this, there are a variety of sensor options:

  • Depth Control – Pressure Sensor: Pressure sensors are a vital component that allow my sub to automatically reach neutral buoyancy and hold depth when switched to the micro sub. Although BlueRobotics sells a pressure sensor, their options are extremely expensive (>$80), and so I will have to use an MS5837-30BA ($12), which is made to be mounted on PCBs.
  • Orientation Control – Inertial Measurement Unit: Another sensor that can help with station hold is an IMU, which can automatically detect acceleration and direction. Although the cheapest and most available option is the MPU-6050, it is pretty noisy, and for this reason, I will be upgrading to an ICM-42688-P ($10).
  • Position Control: Position is the most difficult thing to hold. Below are options:
    • Optical Flow: Using a downward-pointing camera, optical flow can detect the terrain of the sea floor and automatically run algorithms to try to keep the sea floor in place. However, for any inspections that happen far away from the sea floor, high currents, and even slightly murky water, optical flow will be disrupted. The biggest advantage is that it is cheap and a good option for DIYers.
    • Sonar: A great option, Sonars can track the sea floor similar to optical flow and keep a submarine in place, except unlike optical flow, they work in murky waters / farther distances. Even 1D ping sonars can work, making it slightly less expensive but still highly inaccessible. Blue Robotics’ Ping Sonar Altimeter and Echosounder cost over $400. For this reason, I cannot include it on my ROV.
    • Doppler Velocity Logs: A really good device made exactly for station holding it uses acoustic beams and uses the Doppler effect to figure out velocity relative to the sea floor. However, these are EXTREMELY EXPENSIVE, making them inaccessible. Options like the DVL A50 cost >$8000.
    • Tether Tension Sensing: By measuring tether tension at the strain relief, a submarine can try to keep it constant to prevent excessive drift. Although potentially cheap and a good option for DIYers, it is not perfect and can be complex to implement in relation to the tether management system, as cheap TMSs often lead to random tension readings (like if your tether management is done by holding the tether in a milk crate). For its inaccuracy and complexity when integrating discrete floatation devices on the tether (which makes the tether unspoolable), it is eliminated.
    • Bottom Sitting: Instead of relying on tons of sensor data and expensive devices, a simple solution for DIYers is to bottom sit or park the ROV. This means using a ballast / vertical thrusters to keep the ROV stuck to the ground and not moving. Although this is a cheap (free) method, it fails when there are lots of plants, and disturbs the environment (stirring up silt, destroying plants and organisms), ultimately making it a bad option for my ROV, which is meant to operate in an environmentally friendly manner in sensitive environments. It also doesn’t work well for deep water where inspection or surveying needs to happen at a greater altitude, because the micro sub can only be deployed on the floor.
    • Anchors: Bottom sitting has the problem of only being able to deploy the micro sub near the floor, but using a deployable anchor can solve this. However, an automatic anchor is complex, heavy, and doesn’t perfectly hold position, only passively resisting movement. It also has greater ecological concerns than bottom sitting since the anchor scrapes along the floor, destroying plants, organisms, and stirring up silt.
    • Secondary Operator: This is a great option to keep costs low, where a secondary operator can hold the macro sub still, or even somewhat follow the micro sub. However, my goal is to make ROV fleets easier to deploy and reduce the resources needed to operate them. Requiring two operators contradicts this goal. It is also problematic in the design because it requires multiple video feeds and control inputs to be fed to the sub through the tether, meaning PoE trickle charging is not possible. Overall, a great option for DIYers, but not perfect for my specific constraints.
    • Camera Switching: By having a button on the controller lead to instant camera feed switching between the macro and micro sub, I can rely on the IMU acceleration data or optical flow for coarse position control while depth and orientation are autonomously corrected, and every minute or so, I can switch back and check on the macro-sub’s position. This is a great option for my submarine because it is cheap, does not impact the environment, unlike anchors or bottom sitting, and does not require a second operator. It is important to make the switching fast and simple.

Data Collection Systems:

The primary goal of this ROV is to be able to monitor underwater environments in Austin, which have an outbreak of toxic algae, zebra mussels, and an endangered Barton Creek Salamander population. Besides physical inspection surveys, there are a few other things I can consider:

  • Pressure/Depth Sensor: Alongside the collection of any other data, depth data should be included to better understand differences between water layers. This is best achieved through a low-cost pressure sensor such as an MS5837-30BA ($12).
  • Temperature Sensor: Collecting water temperature outside the sub when taking samples and changing position is important to find thermoclines, differences between water layers, and understand environmental factors for problems such as the Barton Creek Salamander, Zebra Mussels, and toxic algae bloom growth. One option is the DS18B20.
  • Dissolved Oxygen Sensors: Although important data for understanding environmental conditions for algae growth, organism habitats, etc., the sensors are extremely expensive, $100+, and cheap ones are inaccurate. The best solution is to take water samples and analyze them.
  • Potentiometric Glass pH Sensors: Although another important factor for understanding environmental conditions during ecological surveys, the sensors are once again expensive, $100+, and large. The best solution is to take water samples and analyze the pH there.
  • Heavy Metal Sensors: An important factor for researching how pollution may be affecting ecosystems, but there aren’t many available sensors. The best solution is to take water samples and analyze heavy metals there; there are problems with heavy metals clinging to container walls. Although I will not focus on heavy metal sensors, TDS sensors (below) can show relative amounts (though not type) of solids, ultimately allowing for further missions to research heavy metals.
  • Total Dissolved Solid Sensors: These can detect minerals, organic materials, and other dissolved solids caused by pollution, an important factor in water quality, especially here in Austin with the common hard water. There are many sensors compatible with microcontrollers that are cheap ($10), making it a viable sensor to include.
  • Turbidity Sensor: These can detect the turbidity of the water, something strongly affected by the organisms I am trying to research; toxic algae blooms increase turbidity while zebra mussels decrease it. They are low-cost, and many can easily be integrated with microcontrollers.
  • Hydrophone: An underwater microphone can be a valuable tool for analyzing things like boat traffic, which affects an ecosystem. They are also low-cost and available to DIYers. The biggest considerations for hydrophones are that although they can analyze boat traffic, they aren’t valuable without long-term tracking and analyzing statistical trends. A low-traffic area could happen to have a boat when your ROV is deployed, and a high-traffic area could happen to have no boats when your ROV is deployed. For this reason, it isn’t valuable for short-mission ROVs like mine, but it is worth considering for long-term monitors.
  • Water Sampling: Researching things like algae, oxygen levels, heavy metals, pH, and bacteria is best done by taking water samples rather than onboard sensors. For this reason, I will be making a very robust water sampling system that can take and store multiple samples automatically. Research indicates that for pH, alkalinity, ORP, turbidity, TDS, and microorganism investigation, you need around 30mL minimum of water for a good reading. Dissolved oxygen and heavy metal research takes 500-2000 mL of water for accurate readings and struggles with the sampling device interfering with readings (particles clinging to the surface), so I will not be focusing on this, especially since the TDS and turbidity sensors can already provide valuable information about pollution.

Cost-Effective Water Sampling Mechanisms:

Since water sampling is the best way to have tons of different tests done on the water, I wanted to be able to collect tons of samples without it becoming extremely expensive. Many designs that came immediately to my head would require 1 servo per sample ($3), which felt costly. I eventually came up with various original ideas:

  • Double Notched Revolving Mechanism: By having a circular perimeter of syringes one motor can both index and trigger the syringes. To save costs, rather than having a pump or motor-actuated syringe, I just went for a spring that will automatically pull it open. Then, to control and index the motor I wanted to use a single central servo/motor. To achieve this I have two notches cut out in the plunger, one at the top, that a servo-controlled disk uses to block the syringe from coming up, and a second at the bottom of the plunger, to allow the servo-controlled disk to rotate through the already-activated syringe. Below is a sketch I made: I plan to use 30mL syringes, being pulled open by 1.5 inch rubber bands (cheap). 30mL have the most reasonable diameter for my application. The system should be submerged in water, meaning waterproofing / buying a waterproof servo. Submerging the system in water is important such that both sides of the syringe (plunger side and internal side) are equalized, and thus we don’t need any pressure rated parts.
  • Preventing Contamination: Syringe based water samplers can struggle with water contamination if measures aren’t taken. Once the syringe is drawn, the correct water fills the inside, but the tip hole stays exposed. This means that as the ROV travels different water can start to diffuse inside / be shoved in by movement. To solve this you can add a plug tied/connected to the plunger. When the plunger extends, the plug is pulled toward the tip, eventually filling the hole.
  • Mounting the Sensors: These sensors uniquely need to be mounted externally to measure the water. To do this, I will either create a custom PCB or custom wiring and insert them into a shallow box; then I will paint the solder joints or the PCB with CorrosionX before inserting them into epoxy. I will specifically be using marine epoxy and making sure that the probes/sensor heads are exposed outside of the epoxy while the circuits are covered.

Emergency Recovery Systems:

Emergency recovery systems are important to both reduce environmental risk and allow one to recover their sub and repair it. I am focusing on emergency recovery because my sub will be operating in sensitive environments, and I have decided to include a Lipo. Most emergencies in the ROV world are 100% solvable:

  • Pressure Implosion: Properly calculate your ROV’s maximum pressure tolerance and do not surpass it. Include a pressure/depth sensor onboard.
  • Slow Water Leak: Properly seal all penetrations and add detectors to find water leaks before they become problematic. Paint circuit boards with an anti-moisture product (CorrosionX) and have an autonomous recovery process where vertical thrusters go to max speed to get the sub to the surface.
  • Immediate Catastrophic Failures: In events like a seal suddenly failing and the hull fills with water, CorrosionX and other products will not be able to save electronics. Furthermore, the added weight will make surfacing difficult even if the motors stay on for some time. To recover from immediate catastrophic failures, the tether should have a very good strain relief system (not just tugging on electrical joints) so that the tether can be used to extract the ROV, either by pulling it up with it or using it as a guide to find it and swim for it (if in shallow water).
  • Communication Loss: In events where the operator loses control of the submarine, such as tether breakage, pressure-induced signal degradation, tether kinks, etc., the microcontroller onboard should detect the signal loss and automatically rise to the surface on its own.
  • Tether Snag: In events where a tether is snagged on something, make sure to have a strong strain relief system on the tether. Do not pull on the tether; instead, guide the ROV backward, using its own tether as a guide. This can be prevented by making sure the tether is neutrally or slightly positively buoyant.
  • Running Out Of Battery: To prevent running out of battery, causing your ROV to be lost, make the tether strong enough to be tugged on by having a strong strain relief. Furthermore, with trickle charging PoE, you can let your battery recharge until you can surface. Adding GUI info about battery level is important to prevent this.
  • Thruster Entanglement: Thruster entanglement, where a propeller becomes clogged, is best fixed with redundancy and shields. Integrating a propeller shroud and fan guard can prevent most clogs. Thruster entanglement is also why I have both a ballast system and vertical thrusters to be able to surface, and why having a 6 thruster array is good; even if the front or back pair gets clogged, you can use the other pair to continue.

MICRO SUB

Hull:

Design:

For more details on hull design, see the Macro sub section. For the micro sub, I was able to get away with CCTV acrylic domes, because their thickness is still 2- 3 mm while being a lot smaller inner diameter. This meant that Under Pressure calculated I can go to 6563.3 feet, giving me a 65x safety factor at 100 feet even if it is thinner than stated in some spots. My main tube for the hull is made out of polycarbonate because it was the easiest to find in comparison to cast acrylic at this size. It is 1.25 in OD, 1 in ID, giving me barely enough space for all my electronics. This size will theoretically let me go in any PVC pipe 1 ½ in nominal size or larger. My end cap will be made out of the same acrylic sheet to save costs.

Size:

The entire sub hull, including the retaining rings should be able to fit in 1 ½ inch schedule 40 PVC, meaning an outer diameter that fits (with tolerance) into 1.610 inches. This will be very difficult. My goal is around 1.5 inches OD for a .1 in tolerance. Ideally it will fit inside a 2 inch PVC very easily while thrust stays pretty efficient. This is the smallest pipe I will likely encounter anywhere in these freshwater bodies of water.

Thrusters:

Function:

  • Size Efficiency: The most important thing for the micro sub is that the thrusters are size efficient. The smaller the micro sub can get without losing control over itself, the more effective it is as a research tool.
  • Power Efficiency: I need to achieve high power efficiency because the micro subs battery needs to be relatively small to fit inside the tube. Therefore I need to spend lots of time designing the propeller to achieve high power to thrust efficiency.

Motors:

I will be using brushless motors since they are waterproof, simpler, and smaller. Since my props will be ~37mm diameter, efficient thrust is 4000-6000 rpm. At 12 volts, that means our kV should be 330-500. The size determines torque. I will be choosing one of the smallest motors offered by APISQUEEN underwater BLDC motors, which are 24mm in diameter.

Size:

The entire device needs to fit within 1.5 inches. This means that multi-thruster arrays won’t work, which isn’t a deal breaker since it mainly needs to steer while turning. Given one rear thruster, with a 2mm thick shroud that means the propeller should fit within a 1.329 inch inner diameter. This is difficult because servos/various mechanisms to steer will struggle to fit. Here are my options:

  • Body Fins + Fixed Thrust: By having internal servos inside the hull transmit motion through magnets, fins outside the tube can control rotation. However it would require 2+ servos, taking up 40-60mm of space within the tube and it would be difficult to make reliable when using magnets through ⅛ inch thick acrylic. However, both the problems are solvable, but the biggest problem is that the OD of the tube is 1.25 inches, meaning the fins could only be ⅛ inch tall, which do not have enough surface area to create any meaningful turning.
  • Pop-Up Body Fins: By having body fins with internal springs that mechanically collapse when the ROV enters a tight tube and pop out when it exits, a greater control surface area can be achieved. However this could only double my height to ¼ inch unless you embedded a pit for extra fins to stay in, inside the tube, which would make it difficult to have the turning mechanism and create issues for pressure resistance.
  • Rudders + Fixed Thrust: Instead of having body fins you could place a rudder in front of the thruster, a common solution by many ROVs. However, the tight dimensions mean fitting a servo is impossible, and even using gears or other power transmission mechanisms it would become complex and bulky.
  • Ballast + Single-Axis Rotating Thrust: By having a ballast to change vertical depth and one servo on the thruster to steer left and right, you can achieve plenty of control. However meaningful ballast systems (10mL+) that use syringes are long (3-4inches+) and require a syringe, a gear rack, an N20, a brushed motor board, and tubing which makes them both space inefficient and expensive. Plus to achieve positional feedback would require an incremental encoder on the back of the N20 (more expensive) as well as a micro limit switch to zero it.
  • Rotating Water Pump + Fixed Thrust: Having a water pump that rotates can achieve slow left/right/up/down motions while the fixed thrust provides main transit. However, the water pump would need to be placed precisely in the center of buoyancy to prevent rotation, meaning splitting the hull in half, which alongside the rotating pump makes cable penetrations and seals impossible.
  • Water Pump + Single-Axis Rotating Thrust: A single axis rotating thrust can achieve left/right turning while a fixed water pump can control vertical depth. However, once again the water pump still needs to be placed in the center of buoyancy to cause translation instead of rotation, which is hard to get perfect and means splitting the hull in half. Although the seals are not dynamic, it is still inefficient and requires a lot more penetrations (failure points + costly), and the water pump is imprecise and slow, and if the sub rotates it could be catastrophic.
  • Tunnel Thruster + Single-Axis Rotating Thrust: With just one servo you can steer left and right without adding much bulk, and with a tunnel thruster you can control vertical thrust. The tunnel thruster is best near the center of buoyancy, which similar to the water pump ideas are problematic as they split the hull in half, decreasing pressure rating while increasing complexity and cost. It also has the issue that if the sub rolls no thruster can correct it.
  • Linear Actuator Vectored Thrust: By having two linear actuators move around a cone behind the motor or move the motor itself, full thrust control can be achieved, and it working in any direction means it's resistant to roll. However the biggest issue is that none of the linear actuators options can be waterproofed easily or are too big. The options include: linear servos (exposed components without a case nor easy way to waterproof them), linear brushed motor actuators (way too large, hard to oil-fill because their internal volume changes, filled with shortable electronics like limit switches, casings have tons of unsealed connected parts), solenoids (utilize an air gap that will implode and is impossible to fix and the coils are often left exposed and able to be shorted).
  • Double Servo Gimbal Vectored Thrust: By having two (waterproofed) servos configured in a gimbal you can move the thruster in any direction achieving vectored thrust. However it is space inefficient and the thruster will both translate and rotate not just tilt, ultimately making controlling it difficult. Servo wires and motor wires also have to move a lot, increasing failure points. Below is an image demonstrating a simple prototype of this.
  • Double Servo Linear Arm Vectored Thrust: Having two side by side servos with arms push a thruster on a ball joint, vectored thrust is achieved, with the motor tilting rather than the previous (worse) translating+tilting, and the servos wires do not move, reducing failure points, while the motor wire does not have to move nearly as much. It is also more space efficient, ultimately making it the best option. Below is an image demonstrating how it works. The arms move linearly but are controlled by rotary servos so that the servo can be waterproofed.

Power:

Here was my original plan, written as if I will do it, and ignoring the issues that voltage regulators that are small, high-current, and support 48V practically don’t exist, and even if they did, the thermal buildup would be dangerous:

PoE

PoE gives the micro sub infinite run time allowing the micro sub to use basically as much power as it may need for as long as inspection is required. It also decreases the weight of the microsub, making it more maneuverable. Achieving it gives us a tricky power situation: ultra-slim CAT6 while using only 2 pairs of conductors for power means we can only send ~1.5 amps over each pair. If we just sent 12v + 2 amps across 2 pairs we would have about 24W before voltage drop over the wire. This is not enough to power everything onboard. Here is the plan: Voltage Boosting on Supply Side: The macro subs 3S lipo produces 11.1 volts, but with a step-up converter we can make it 48volts, the highest safe voltage, which means our power at 1 amp is now 48W. Most high amperage step up converters are potted in epoxy, which conveniently makes them waterproof. Fuse: 48V is the highest voltage labeled as “low-voltage,” and if any connection fails on the 48V tether, it will lead to more violent electrolysis. For this reason, we need to incorporate a fuse, but unfortunately our load is non-constant. We do not want the fuse to blow if we shortly spike to 3A when running the ballast motor + thruster. For this reason, I will be incorporating a 2A slow-blow fuse. It won’t blow with short inrushes, only sustained high amperage. Voltage Step-Down on Micro Sub: Receiving 48V, the micro sub will step it down to 5V, which will run all of the electronics from the motors to the microcontroller. This gives us ~10 amps to utilize at 5 volts.

Why PoE Fails

Although it could technically work, it would cost way too much ($50-100) while having issues with thermal buildup and underpowering. Most buck converters can only handle 3 amps, making powering a brushless motor impossible, and buying a higher amperage one that can fit in the 1 in ID tubing is insanely expensive, large, and won’t have enough heat dissipation inside the ROV, ultimately being dangerous, potentially causing automatic heat shutoff, or the hull blowing open. Below is my new plan:

BatteriesChosen

Implementing a LiPo battery is a lot cheaper as it bypasses 3+ expensive ultra-small voltage regulators and all the issues that come with them like heat buildup and low current. The main downside is that run time is now limited, but using an efficient design it can be unproblematic. With a LiPo Stick battery (long with a small cross section), you can fit plenty of power. For my design, around 6 inches long with a 1 in ID, a 3S 1000mAh battery can fit, providing 12Wh of power, giving me 30+ mins of active movement runtime, while its rectangular design still allows 4mm diameter cables to pass through the hull unlike if you had used cylindrical Li Ion batteries.

SURFACE TETHER MANAGEMENT SYSTEM

Surface tether management systems can become extremely expensive without providing much useful differences. The most common choices are:

  • Spools: Spools are simple and available, but when they carry an electrical cable, they need a slip ring. 8-wire slip rings (for CAT6) cost around $20 for a low-power one. If your setup uses PoE in any capacity, the best option is likely to avoid spools. Spools can also be tricky because discrete floats can get in the way of a nice uniform coil and the slip ring is a potentially catastrophic point of failure.
  • Milk Crates: By holding the tether inside a bin like a milk crate, and making a simple roller system to deploy it smoothly passively, discrete floats do not become a nuisance, and the system becomes cheap and reliable. Unlike the underwater tether management system, it does not need to be motorized since tether management can be easily done by a second helper, or by only bringing it back in once the ROV has finished its mission and returned to the surface, where the one operator no longer needs to control it, and can use their hands to grab the tether. These are cheap, work well for most missions, and you don't lose that many benefits of spools.

Acetone Vapor Smoothing:

By placing a paper towel soaked with acetone inside a chamber with a print, the vapors react with ABS and melt the outside edge into one smooth, glossy surface. Once the print is removed, the acetone evaporates and the plastic rehardens. Here are details: Use strong, pure acetone and soak a paper towel. Get your chamber, which can be a big metal container, non-acetone-reacting plastic (be careful it doesn’t react), glass, etc. Put the paper towel down on the ground, then get something to raise your 3D print off of the surface of the paper towel, like an upside-down small bowl of non-reactive material. Place the print on top of the stand-off, and then cover it with the chamber. The acetone will evaporate and make fumes, filling the chamber and reacting with the ABS. To reiterate, the plastic must be ABS. Check on it frequently, and the whole process should take 10-20 mins for small parts, 20-45 for medium parts, and 45+ for larger. Choosing a clear chamber material can help you check on the part while it vapor smooths; look for all the layer lines gone and a glossy surface, and do not wait until it starts to lose detail/warp; that is over vapor smoothing and causing deformation. Do this whole process in a well-ventilated place, like outside (though be careful of dust getting in the slightly melted ABS surface), because acetone is extremely harmful to breath in and is extremely flammable.

3D Printing Information:

  • Infill And Buoyancy: All 3D printed parts should be printed with 100% infill, or have their inner cavity filled with something hard like epoxy, otherwise water / air will fill it randomly, changing buoyancy uncontrollably.
  • Water Through Layer Lines: Water can travel through the super small gaps formed at layer lines. If your 3D print crosses a wet-dry boundary in any way it needs to be waterproofed. Your options are adding epoxy across the surface or, if you use ABS, vapor smoothing (see section: Acetone Vapor Smoothing).
