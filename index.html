SOSORUM Separable Operated Submersible for Observation, Rescue, and Underwater Monitoring

An open-source ROV design guide born from Austin's water crises — built to make underwater research and exploration accessible to anyone, not just those with a $5,000+ budget.

FRONT MATTER / 01

Methodology Note

This document is a comprehensive component selection guide, not a fixed blueprint for one ROV configuration. Every DIY builder has a unique budget, mission, and fabrication capability — so rather than handing over "the correct answer," this Basis of Design systematically works through the pros, cons, and trade-offs of the major subsystems: hull materials, end-cap sealing, sensor payloads, thrusters, and tether management.

It includes the rationale behind SOSORUM's specific choices, but those choices aren't always the right ones for every project. The goal is to hand over the engineering rationale needed to mix and match components into a submersible that fits your own mission — infrastructure inspection, deep-sea work, or a micro ROV.

Written intentionally plain This document is written informally on purpose, to stay accessible to the common person rather than sound "smart." No images in this document are sourced externally — all are original (3D design software, real demonstrations, sketches). No AI was used in the creation of this document or any supporting media, graphs, or calculations.
FRONT MATTER / 02

Project Goals

Three goals drive every trade-off in this document: cost accessibility, a local ecological crisis, and a new architecture for ROV deployment.

01

Making ROVs Accessible

Earth's waters stay unexplored while ROVs are gatekept behind expensive parts and hard-to-find information. "Affordable" solutions today — like Blue Robotics' BlueROV2 — still cost upward of $5,000. A high-quality, lower-cost ROV, documented openly, opens the water to more research, surveying, protection, mapping, and search-and-rescue — and lets larger fleets deploy without extra cost. Accessibility also means the document itself has to be readable: written like a normal, understandable human, not slung with jargon.

02

Help Austin's Water Crises

Having lived in Austin for years, the water crises are personal: paddleboarding above water too polluted to swim in because of toxic algae blooms, boiling water for days from bacteria in the supply, swimming in the same pool that is the exclusive habitat of a salamander species slowly going extinct, and cutting feet on the zebra mussels clogging infrastructure. SOSORUM is meant to make ecological surveys easy to run — tracking bacteria and algae at any depth, surveying and helping restore endangered species, and monitoring invasive ones.

03

Pioneer Multi-ROV / Micro-ROV Systems

Micro submarines open up monitoring of pipes and cramped spaces, but they're fundamentally limited: low power, low electronics capability, slow speed. Docking a micro sub onto a macro sub solves those problems — power, electronics, and long transit distances are handled by the larger vehicle. Micro subs also help in search-and-rescue, where rubble blocks boxy subs like BlueRobotics'; the catch is SAR needs speed while ROV investigation needs endurance, which somewhat rules micro subs out for SAR — but it demonstrates the macro-micro concept for a future SAR-focused project. SOSORUM itself is catered to ecological work rather than SAR.

Environmental Harm

SOSORUM operates in environments highly sensitive to pollution — including the exclusive habitat of the Barton Creek Salamander — so a non-immediately-recoverable crash (one requiring SCUBA to retrieve) is treated as a critical risk. Several materials are restricted or banned outright:

BatteriesRestricted

Lithium-ion batteries are standard in the ROV world but can release toxic pollution if left to corrode after a crash. Alternatives: supply power over the tether, or build a guaranteed emergency recovery system (see Emergency Recovery Systems).

Hydraulic FluidsProhibited

Traditional hydraulic fluid is oil-based and causes large-scale environmental damage if it leaks — in any application. Fully banned, with no emergency-system workaround. Alternative: water as a hydraulic fluid, paired with non-rustable parts.

LeadProhibited (excl. trace <0.1% wt)

Can dissolve into water and cause harm. Alternatives: lead-free solder, steel or tin weights.

MACRO SUB / 01

Tether

The tether has to carry power, data, and video over 60+ meters while staying flexible, waterproof, and (ideally) neutrally buoyant — and every jacket material trades one of those off.

Jacket Material Options

Blue Robotics Tether (CAT6)

The original plan — but at $450+, with power struggling since it's CAT6-sized (1A max). Neutrally buoyant and waterproof, but that buoyancy is tuned for seawater, so it may run slightly positive in freshwater.

LLDPE CAT6

Better waterproofing for greater depth, but too inflexible for maneuverability and surface spooling. Sourced scrap from a satellite showed it cantilevering 2+ feet off a workbench unsupported, and holding twists/spirals instead of forming a natural catenary curve.

Silicone CAT6

High waterproofing and flexibility — the most appealing option on paper — but expensive, hard to source, and prone to kinking and damage.

PVC (with HDPE inner) CAT6Chosen

The most abundant and affordable option. PVC jackets are semi-permeable, so long, multi-day submersion lets water work through micropores — but the inner conductors are jacketed in fully waterproof HDPE, so as long as that inner jacket has no flaws, it holds to deep depths. Water will still make it down the tether toward the penetrator, so wire ends need to be sealed properly with a high-quality penetrator like WetLink. Chosen because it saves $100 versus PUR jacket, while full LLDPE is too stiff.

TPE CAT6

Similar properties to LLDPE but flexible — waterproof, pressure-rated, durable, maneuverable. Competitive, but rarity pushed pricing to absurd levels for runs over 100 feet (versus ~$1/ft for common options).

PUR CAT6Selected material

Landed on this. Expensive (~$0.60/ft), but unmatched durability, flexibility, and waterproofing for deeper depth. Common in robotics, so it's actually available.

Jacket Style

Round

Common, equal drag and flexibility in all directions — the right call for a flexible jacket material. Most penetrators (like Blue Robotics' WaterLink) are made for round cable.

Flat

Less common but available, allowing more flexibility with stiffer materials like LLDPE. Bends non-uniformly and drag varies with orientation. Standard round-cable penetrators don't fit it.

Pressure-Induced Signal Degradation

PUR's anti-kinking material helps prevent signal loss, but signal loss isn't only caused by kinking — high pressure collapses the jacket too. Filling the CAT6 with oil or gel prevents that, but doing it DIY is tricky, and off-the-shelf gel-filled cable becomes nearly as expensive as the Blue Robotics tether. Pressure-induced degradation on stock CAT6 tends to start around 45 meters. SOSORUM's freshwater use case tops out at 30 meters, under that threshold.

Tether Buoyancy

The Blue Robotics tether's biggest advantage is being neutrally buoyant in saltwater — any regular CAT6 is negatively buoyant. Pressure-rated foam floats can correct that, but must actually be pressure-rated or they collapse/release bubbles and sink. Discrete floats also snag on plants underwater and make surface spooling messy. SOSORUM uses closed-cell rubber nitrile pipe insulation, cut into 1-inch segments and spaced along the cable — figured by physical calculation and confirmed with underwater tests.

Wire Core & Water Wicking

Copper-Clad Aluminum

Cheaper, fine for low-amperage/low-speed use — but the high voltage drop and low amperage capacity rule it out for PoE.

CopperChosen

More expensive but necessary for PoE — lower resistance means less voltage drop and higher safe amperage without overheating.

Solid Core

Cheaper, and prevents water from wicking through like a straw — but extremely inflexible, which hurts a maneuverable sub's tether.

Braided CoreChosen

Flexible, but allows water wicking — a nick or exposed wet end can travel water down the wire into the hull and fry electronics. A double-insulated cable like CAT6 helps, so it takes a more extreme failure to cause real damage. End sealing at every penetration still matters.

Capsule Penetration

WetLink Penetrator

Slightly expensive ($13–17) but a clean, well-documented, superior-pressure-rated way to pass cable from wet to dry. Downsides: cost, limited cable compatibility, and availability (mainly Blue Robotics).

Potted PenetratorsChosen

Cheaper (~$5), less strength/waterproofing than WetLink, but simple, affordable, available — and still provides structural support against implosion (unlike direct epoxy). Requires epoxy that bonds well with the chosen insulation.

Direct Epoxy

Fanning each conductor out and epoxying it makes a cheap watertight seal, but it's hard to do well, can short, and can't handle deeper depth. PUR specifically bonds poorly to epoxy — worse after repeated bend cycles — and fanning a full CAT6 without conductors touching is difficult. Not used for the tether, but valid for motor wires and small endcaps like the Micro Sub's, where the smaller size lowers implosion risk.

MACRO SUB / 02

Power

Powering a strong main thruster array, camera servos, ballast motors, and data collection at once means a surprisingly high amperage budget — solved across three chronological pivots.

Power Systems

Full PoE (Power over Ethernet)

Lets the sub run indefinitely on one cord — but CAT6 only handles ~0.96A per wire pair. With at least 4 pairs needed for control (and possibly data), only 0.9–1.8A of power remains — nowhere near enough for a sub that needs to move at moderate speed.

Cable Bundling

Lower-gauge wire raises the amperage limit, but combined with CAT6 comm wire it gets expensive fast — especially in pure copper, which doesn't fit a low budget. Any above-water power run also fights resistance (R = ρL/A); over 200+ feet that forces a higher supply voltage and an onboard regulator, which drags batteries back into the picture. The thicker bundle also hurts maneuverability, weighs the sub down, and makes surface tether management much harder.

Battery Chemistry

NiMH

Safer and more stable than lithium — but low energy density and a very low discharge rating make it unsuitable for strong (especially brushless) motors.

NiCd

Similarly weak energy density and discharge rating to NiMH, without the environmental upside — cadmium is toxic.

Li-ion

Good energy density and discharge rating, but carries a similar environmental hazard to LiPo without the added density — not ideal where weight and size matter.

LiPoChosen

High discharge rating and energy density; the trade-off is lower stability and environmental hazard. Because SOSORUM uses LiPo, an emergency recovery system is mandatory per the environmental constraint (see Emergency Recovery Systems).

Trickle-Charging PoE — considered, not used Dedicating 1–2 CAT6 pairs to power while running an onboard battery could extend runtime and slowly recharge/limp the ROV home if the battery is mismanaged. But it adds electronic complexity, dangerous heat buildup, and cost that isn't worth the small benefit for a budget build.

Battery Capacity & Voltage

Capacity is a trade-off between run length and how sluggish the sub feels — weight barely affects top speed (drag dominates underwater) but does slow acceleration. DIY ROVs typically run 5,000–20,000 mAh; the BlueROV2 uses an 18,000 mAh pack for ~2 hours of runtime. Two 8,000 mAh packs in parallel saved SOSORUM over $40 versus one larger pack, since battery price grows faster than capacity — this needs a parallel balance board. For voltage, a 3S 11.1V LiPo was chosen to match a 350kV motor with 70mm props at the recommended 3,000–4,000 RPM (11.1V × 350kV ≈ 3,885 RPM).

Battery C-Rating

C-rating sets the max continuous amp output: a 3 Ah (3,000 mAh) 3S LiPo that needs to supply 60A continuous needs at least a 20C rating (3 Ah × 20C = 60A). Most LiPos handle this fine; C-rating mainly becomes an issue with very high amperage draws or very small packs. NiMH C-ratings, by comparison, run low (often unlisted, 1–5C) and are hard to work with.

Battery Bay

For an open-source, multi-purpose ROV, the battery bay matters more than usual: interchangeable sleeves let builders swap in different battery brands/capacities (SOSORUM's sleeve fits 2× 8,000 mAh, but swapping the sleeve accommodates 2× 9,000–10,000 mAh or one 20,000 mAh pack). Thermal dissipation is also worth designing for — especially with trickle-charging PoE or a racing build — via heat sinks, open air paths, fans, or thermometers.

Thrusters

Functions

Transit — moving between survey sites without the micro sub deployed; wants speed and easy control. Station hold — autonomously holding position while the micro sub is deployed, using the sensors covered in Station Holding Systems.

Thruster Array — 6-Thruster Layout

Full directional authority for station hold also makes the sub easier to drive and more maneuverable — worth the extra complexity over a simple transit-only layout. Two vertical thrusters sit near the center of buoyancy for fine vertical control and fast emergency surfacing; four edge thrusters point 45° inward to cover forward/back, left/right, and yaw.

No pitch authority This layout doesn't give pitch control — that needs an 8-thruster setup, which costs more for little gain. Pitch is passively handled by careful weight balance during design; a properly balanced ROV should sit level without active control. A camera servo achieves the one real benefit of pitch (viewing angle) more cheaply, and also helps with optical-flow station holding. An alternative for true pitch control: sliding a heavy component (like the battery) on a servo or leadscrew to shift the center of buoyancy. This layout also keeps the front/back view unobstructed by thrusters, so the camera has a clean line of sight and the tether can't clip a propeller.

Thruster Motors

Brushed

Internal brush wears out over time (shorter life, more maintenance) but costs less upfront and is simpler to control with just voltage changes. Fully submersible, though the bearing isn't waterproof and will need replacing. SOSORUM would use RS 540/550 (RS 380/390 also common) at this scale.

BrushlessChosen

No physical contact inside — longer life, lower maintenance, more power-efficient, but higher amperage draw and a more complex ESC. ROV brushless motors should generally run under 700 kV (most often 300–500 kV). Fully submersible, bearing still needs replacing, and wires should be epoxy-treated for longevity.

Motor Location & Power Transmission

Magnetically Coupled

Opposing-polarity magnet discs transmit power through a dry wall to a wet propeller — keeps the motor dry (no penetrators, no bearing replacement, no wire wicking) but efficient designs are hard to get right; many basic builds end up weak.

Wet-MountedChosen

Common for brushless motors — placed directly in the water since the motor (besides the bearing) is already waterproof. Low friction, no extra parts, efficient and cheap. Motor contacts need proper sealing and cord ends need to be protected from wicking.

Propeller Housing

Kort-Style Nozzle

Hydrodynamically shaped shroud that boosts thrust with a wing-like cross section — but only optimized in one direction, making it a poor fit for bidirectional ROV thrusters.

Cylindrical ShroudChosen

Not optimized for maximum one-way thrust, but performs best bidirectionally — the right trade for an ROV.

Propellers

Bought propellers are strong and high quality but hard to size for a given motor, and ROV-specific ones are expensive. 3D-printed propellers save cost and can be optimized for the exact setup, but need enough strength for high RPM (SOSORUM prints in ABS, 100% infill, then vapor-smooths with acetone — see Construction) and a smooth surface for efficiency. ROV propellers want smaller pitch than drone propellers, since water is far more viscous — and bidirectional props generally beat unidirectional ones, since relying on a single motor's rotation to reverse thrust feels sluggish underwater. Design tools worth using: OpenProp, OpenThruster, a build-your-own test rig, and community references (BlueRobotics, Homebuilt ROVs, r/ROV). A pitch calculator like boatcalculators.com.au helps dial in efficiency.

ESCs / Motor Controllers

Brushed motors use cheap, common H-bridge bidirectional controllers that vary output voltage to set speed. Brushless motors need a proper ESC — pricier, and bidirectional ones aren't always labeled as such (sometimes it's a software feature). Since ROVs usually run 4+ motors, multi-motor ESC boards (commonly 4-in-1) are the more economical choice.

ESC redundancy Splitting multi-motor ESCs deliberately preserves control if one fails. SOSORUM links the front two motors + one vertical thruster on one ESC, and the back two motors + the other vertical thruster on a second ESC — so a single ESC failure still leaves forward/back, yaw, and partial up/down control for recovery.
MACRO SUB / 04

Video System

Analog vs. digital: digital needs many more conductors (MIPI-style cabling), which is both hard to source at tether length and prone to interference over 200+ feet — analog is the only realistic choice here.

Servo-controlled camera: a single ~$3 servo under the camera lets the pilot look left/right from the controller — cheap and worthwhile. A second servo gives full pan/tilt, but becomes hard to control without head tracking.

Interference & baluns: running 200+ feet of analog video alongside other tether signals invites static. A balun transforms the signal pair into opposing (positive/negative) copies of the same signal plus noise; the topside balun then subtracts the two to cancel out interference that hit both lines equally, leaving a clean image.

Multi-sub video switching: switching between macro- and micro-sub cameras after deployment needs a camera-switching board that ties into both cameras' analog signals and a PWM line from the microcontroller for remote control input.

MACRO SUB / 05

Buoyancy

Near-neutral buoyancy keeps the sub stable and saves battery — slightly positive buoyancy means a communication loss or battery mismanagement still ends with the sub floating up for recovery.

Achieving Neutral Buoyancy

Closed-Cell Foam

Each bubble is sealed off individually, resisting moisture (unlike PVC). Slowly absorbs water over long dives — solvable with waterproof tape. Inexpensive (found in pool noodles), but compresses at depth, reducing buoyancy and demanding more from thrusters/ballast.

Hollow Pipes (PVC)Common DIY choice

Sufficiently pressure-resistant (especially schedule 80) and easy to fabricate, with buoyancy fine-tunable by cutting/rearranging pipe length. Biggest risk: catastrophic failure — a failed end seal or implosion sinks the ROV. Thicker, pressure-rated pipe (checked with Under Pressure software) extends the depth this works at.

Syntactic Foam

Glass/plastic micro-balloons suspended in epoxy — incompressible and rated for real depth, but expensive and a poor fit for a DIY budget.

Ballasts vs. Vertical Thrusters

Active ballasts work on real submarines but aren't efficient for ROVs — once they take on water to adjust buoyancy, they have to haul that water around for the rest of the dive, and getting to a target depth needs a tricky predictive algorithm. Ballasts also fight water pressure directly: a 100 mL syringe piston ballast faces 62 lbs / 28.12 kg of resisting force at 100 ft, and 203 lbs / 92.08 kg at 100 m (328 ft) — which pushes toward high-torque motors, leadscrews, and worm gears, all slow mechanisms fighting a pilot trying to make quick depth changes. Syringe plungers and walls also struggle to survive deep pressure, and upgrading to a more pressure-resistant piston system gets costly fast. The only real upside of active ballast is slight power savings over continuously-running vertical thrusters — outweighed here by complexity, difficulty piloting, and poor pressure resistance. Vertical thrusters — chosen for exactly this reason.

MACRO SUB / 06

Dry Hull

Hull Wall Material

SOSORUM's dry hull is a 4-inch acrylic tube with 0.25-inch walls, 12 inches long — sized to fit a large battery comfortably.

Aluminum

Best for ultra-high-depth work — superior pressure resistance and durability. Drawbacks: cost, availability, and no way to visually inspect for water ingress.

AcrylicChosen — main tube

Cheap and available, but less pressure-resistant than aluminum (not recommended past 100 m). The upside: water ingress is visible. Can be tricky to machine (prone to cracking), but deforms gradually under pressure rather than imploding or shattering outright — a good failure mode for an ROV.

PolycarbonateChosen — micro sub tube

More expensive, still inspectable, higher impact rating than acrylic (though often less optically clear). Useful where the hull might bump rocks or use flat panels. Can occasionally be cheaper to source than cast acrylic — SOSORUM's micro sub hull uses polycarbonate because cast acrylic wasn't easily available at its small OD.

PVC

Cheap and versatile, fine for shallow-water builds — but not for deep diving, and like aluminum, ingress can't be visually inspected. Easier to machine than acrylic.

3D-Printed Plastics

Should not be used for pressure enclosures — poor pressure performance and inconsistent prints lead to premature failure. Print lines are semi-permeable, so any wet/dry boundary print needs full epoxy coverage or water will ingress through the material itself, and must be printed with zero infill air gaps (100% solid, or epoxy-filled) or the part will implode or fill with water. Example: a ½-inch-thick pure-ABS flat endcap failed at 139.54 ft in Under Pressure software — with a 3× safety factor, that's a 46 ft-rated part.

End Caps

Dome

CCTV Acrylic Dome

"2–3mm" acrylic with great optical clarity — but often not real cast acrylic, and thickness can taper from 4mm at the base to 1mm near the top, quietly lowering the real pressure rating. Fine for shallow depths, not deep water. An internal structural frame was initially considered but rejected: frames need gaskets to avoid pressure points that actually cause premature implosion.

Blue Robotics Pressure-Rated DomeChosen

Polycarbonate, genuinely pressure-rated, better impact resistance, made specifically for ROVs. Main con: expensive, and its proprietary O-ring system only really works with their full enclosure ($400+) — SOSORUM uses the dome but designs its own sealing system for cost and engineering practice.

Flat

Blue Robotics Flat Endcaps

Great quality across materials (aluminum, acrylic, etc.), but same issue as the dome — expensive, and the O-ring system assumes the full enclosure system.

DIY Sheet EndcapsChosen

A sheet of material cut to size, with a custom sealing mechanism. Material and thickness choice should weigh machinability, cost, and a pressure rating check (with safety factor) in Under Pressure software.

Electronic Tray

Electronics mount to an easy-to-remove tray, typically steel standoffs/rods or threaded rods running the length of the hull with mounting plates and cable management. It should stay fully separate from the hull (aside from the end cap) so it lifts out cleanly. Organizing by signal flow (e.g. tether-facing boards near the tether penetrator, working toward the microcontroller) and separating noisy electronics (ESCs) from sensitive ones (video components) on opposite sides of the tray keeps interference down.

Wire Selection

Jacket material matters for potted-penetrator compatibility — it needs to bond with epoxy while staying waterproof. PVC (outer) + HDPE (inner) was chosen for cost, waterproofing, and good epoxy bonding; PUR, PVC, and neoprene all bond well, while silicone, PE, and PP struggle. SOSORUM standardizes on 18 AWG 3-conductor cable (6mm OD, matching CAT6 for shared penetrators — 3 conductors covers brushless motors, servos, and brushed motors) and CAT6 for data boards and tethers (lower amperage, but 8 conductors per penetrator). Dupont cables handle low-current internal wiring, though bare pin headers aren't recommended alone for anything critical — they can fail to make contact even when plugged in.

MACRO SUB / 07

Cable Cram — Micro Sub Deployment

Locking Mechanism

The Micro Sub Bay sits mid-underside of the Macro Sub, where running wet-to-dry wiring for a lock isn't practical — every penetrator costs $14+. The fix is transmitting motion magnetically through the wall instead. Electromagnets stay powered the whole time they hold, wasting energy; a servo holding a permanent magnet in range keeps the micro sub docked until it's driven far enough away to break the magnetic connection.

Tether Management

No off-the-shelf tether management system fit, so this is an original design.

Spool

The standard approach — but needs a slip ring, and a pressure-resistant one runs around $4,000. Without that requirement, a spool would otherwise be the simplest option.

Passive Cable Storage (figure-8 / helical coil)

Sidesteps the slip ring — the ROV pulls tether out as it drives, like pulling yarn from a ball. But it only works one direction: once out, it can't be put back, so the micro sub could only ever deploy once.

CableCramOriginal design

Rather than fighting for a uniform internal coil state (which needs an expensive slip ring or an overcomplicated figure-8 rewind), CableCram tolerates randomness: an oversized flooded bucket holds the loose tether, and two gripping wheels (at least one motorized) pull cable in or out through the bay wall. The bucket doesn't need to be pressure-resistant since it's flooded, so it adds little weight, and can be 3D-printed into a low-drag shape. Plans for 10–25 ft of ultra-slim cable — enough for the micro sub as a short-range extension of the macro sub rather than an independent long-range vehicle.

Motor

Motorized to take up slack and allow redeployment. Low RPM needed to match the micro sub's slow feed rate — a geared-down RS 370, driven by a Pololu DRV8874, was the most efficient fit.

Position Knowledge

Friction Clutch

Slips at the end of travel to protect the tether from damage — but gives the microcontroller no feedback, and small in/out struggles can trigger it unnoticed, risking system failure.

Current Sensing (DRV8833)Chosen

End-stop discs placed where the tether physically can't pull further cause an amperage spike the DRV8833 detects and reports, letting the microcontroller mark tether start/end automatically.

Active vs. Passive CableCram

An actively-managed tether (deploying at the same rate the micro sub drives away) was the original plan, but it needs a waterproof encoder (~$1,000 for submersible units) and a tension-measurement system that's both complex and hard to waterproof. For a 25 ft tether, that cost isn't justified. The chosen design is fully passive in/out: pressing deploy feeds tether out at roughly the micro sub's normal speed with no active feedback; pressing retract pulls tether in at the motor's max speed, since the micro sub should already be back at the bay when that's pressed.

MACRO SUB / 08

Station Holding Systems

When the micro sub deploys, the macro sub has to hold roughly still. Depth and orientation are relatively easy; position is the hard part.

Depth Control

Pressure sensor — needed for automatic neutral buoyancy and depth hold. Blue Robotics' options run over $80; SOSORUM uses an MS5837-30BA (~$12), a PCB-mount sensor.

Orientation Control

IMU — the cheap, common MPU-6050 is fairly noisy, so SOSORUM upgrades to an ICM-42688-P (~$10).

Position Control

Optical Flow

A downward camera tracks sea-floor texture to hold position — cheap and DIY-friendly, but disrupted by high altitude above the floor, strong currents, or murky water.

Sonar

Works in murky water and at range, unlike optical flow — but even a 1D ping sonar (Blue Robotics' Ping Sonar Altimeter/Echosounder) costs $400+, ruling it out on budget.

Doppler Velocity Log

Acoustic-beam velocity tracking relative to the sea floor — excellent for station hold, but units like the DVL A50 cost $8,000+.

Tether Tension Sensing

Measuring tension at the strain relief could hold position indirectly, but readings get noisy with a simple tether management setup (like a milk crate), and integrating it clashes with the discrete tether floats that make the cable unspoolable otherwise.

Bottom Sitting

Free, simple — park on the bottom using ballast/thrusters. Fails around plants, disturbs the environment (stirred silt, crushed organisms), and only works when surveying near the floor. Ruled out on environmental grounds.

Deployable Anchor

Solves the floor-only limitation of bottom sitting, but is complex, heavy, only passively resists movement, and scrapes the floor — worse ecological impact than bottom sitting.

Secondary Operator

A second person holds/follows with the macro sub — low-cost and effective for most DIYers, but conflicts with SOSORUM's goal of easy single-operator fleet deployment, and needs a second video/control link over the tether, which rules out PoE trickle charging.

Camera SwitchingChosen

A controller button instantly swaps between macro- and micro-sub camera feeds. IMU acceleration data and optical flow handle coarse macro-sub position while depth/orientation self-correct autonomously; the pilot glances back to the macro-sub view every minute or so to check position. Cheap, no environmental impact, no second operator — fits SOSORUM's constraints directly.

Water Sampling

Algae, oxygen, heavy metals, pH, and bacteria are all better studied from samples than onboard sensors. Roughly 30 mL is enough for pH, alkalinity, ORP, turbidity, TDS, and microorganism testing; dissolved oxygen and heavy metals need 500–2,000 mL and are prone to interference from particles clinging to the sampler — outside SOSORUM's scope, since TDS and turbidity already flag pollution trends.

Double-Notched Revolving Mechanism

To avoid a $3 servo per sample, SOSORUM uses one central servo/motor to both index and trigger a circular ring of 30mL syringes (the most reasonable diameter for the application), each pulled open by a 1.5-inch rubber band instead of a pump or motor-actuated plunger. Two notches cut into each plunger — one at the top (blocks the syringe from opening until indexed) and one at the bottom (lets the disk rotate past an already-triggered syringe) — let a single rotating disk control the whole ring. The system runs fully submerged so both sides of each syringe equalize pressure, meaning none of it needs to be pressure-rated.

Preventing Contamination

Once a syringe draws its sample, the tip hole stays open, letting different water diffuse in or get pushed in by motion. A plug tied to the plunger solves this — as the plunger extends, the plug is pulled toward the tip and eventually seals the hole.

Mounting External Sensors

Pressure, temperature, turbidity, and TDS sensors need direct water contact. Each is built on a custom PCB or wiring, coated with CorrosionX on the solder joints/PCB, then set in marine epoxy with only the probe/sensor head left exposed.

MICRO SUB / 01

Hull

CCTV acrylic domes work at this scale — the thickness is still 2–3mm but the inner diameter is much smaller, and Under Pressure calculated a fail depth of 6,563.3 ft, giving a 65× safety factor at 100 ft even accounting for thin spots. The main tube is polycarbonate (easier to source than cast acrylic at this diameter): 1.25 in OD, 1 in ID — barely enough room for the electronics. The end cap reuses the same acrylic sheet as the macro sub's, to save cost.

Target size: roughly 1.5 in OD with a 0.1 in tolerance, including retaining rings, so the whole hull fits inside 1½-inch schedule 40 PVC (1.610 in with tolerance) — the smallest pipe diameter expected in these freshwater environments — while still fitting easily inside 2-inch PVC with efficient thrust.

MICRO SUB / 02

Thrusters

Everything has to fit inside a 1.5-inch tube while still steering — the real design constraint of the whole micro sub.

Motor

Brushless, for waterproofing, simplicity, and size. At a ~37mm prop diameter, efficient thrust sits at 4,000–6,000 RPM; at 12V that means a 330–500 kV motor. SOSORUM uses one of APISQUEEN's smallest underwater BLDC motors, 24mm in diameter.

Steering Mechanism — Options Considered

A single rear thruster inside a ~1.329-inch shroud ID rules out multi-thruster arrays, so steering has to come from somewhere else. Eight approaches were evaluated:

Body Fins + Fixed Thrust

Servos transmit motion through magnets to external fins. Needs 2+ servos (40–60mm of internal space) and reliable magnetic coupling through ⅛" acrylic — but at 1.25" OD the fins could only stand ⅛" tall, too little surface area to steer meaningfully.

Pop-Up Body Fins

Spring-loaded fins collapse for tight tubes and pop out otherwise — could double fin height to ¼", but embedding a recessed pit for them complicates both the steering mechanism and pressure resistance.

Rudders + Fixed Thrust

A common ROV solution, but the tight dimensions make fitting even a small servo — let alone gearing — impossible without excess bulk.

Ballast + Single-Axis Rotating Thrust

A servo-steered thruster plus a ballast for depth — but a meaningful (10 mL+) syringe ballast needs 3–4+ inches of length, a gear rack, an N20, a driver board, and tubing, plus an encoder and limit switch for position feedback. Too large and too expensive for the space.

Rotating Water Pump + Fixed Thrust

A rotating pump could give slow multi-axis motion, but it must sit exactly at the center of buoyancy to avoid unwanted rotation — meaning splitting the hull in half, which makes penetrations and seals effectively impossible.

Water Pump + Single-Axis Rotating Thrust

Same center-of-buoyancy placement problem as above — still needs a split hull, more penetrations (more failure points, more cost), and the pump itself is imprecise and slow; a sub roll here could be catastrophic.

Tunnel Thruster + Single-Axis Rotating Thrust

One servo steers left/right, a tunnel thruster handles vertical — but the tunnel thruster still wants to sit near the center of buoyancy (splitting the hull, lowering pressure rating, raising cost/complexity), and gives no roll correction.

Linear Actuator Vectored Thrust

Two linear actuators moving a thrust cone around the motor would give full vectoring and roll resistance — but no small linear actuator option waterproofs cleanly: exposed linear servos, oversized linear brushed actuators with internal volume changes that resist oil-filling, or solenoids with an air gap that implodes under pressure.

Double Servo Gimbal Vectored Thrust

Two waterproofed servos in a gimbal give full vectored thrust — but the mechanism both translates and rotates the thruster rather than just tilting it, making control harder, and moving servo/motor wires add failure points. Space-inefficient at this scale.

Double Servo Linear Arm Vectored ThrustChosen — original solution

Two side-by-side rotary servos push linear arms against a ball-jointed thruster mount, tilting the motor without translating it — cleaner control than the gimbal version. Servo wires stay stationary (fewer failure points) and the motor wire barely has to move. More space-efficient overall. The breakthrough was realizing the two servo rotation axes didn't need to be perpendicular to each other, which had been the assumed (and spatially inefficient) constraint.

MICRO SUB / 03

Power

PoE plan — abandoned The original plan boosted the macro sub's 11.1V LiPo to 48V (the highest "low-voltage" safe limit) over the ultra-slim CAT6 tether, protected by a 2A slow-blow fuse to tolerate short current spikes without nuisance-tripping, then stepped down to 5V onboard for ~10A at the micro sub. In practice, small high-current 48V-capable step-down converters that fit the 1-inch ID tube practically don't exist — and even if they did, thermal buildup in a sealed hull would be dangerous. Most buck converters cap around 3A, not enough for a brushless thruster motor, and a higher-amperage unit would be expensive, oversized, and unable to dissipate heat safely — risking automatic shutoff or the hull venting under pressure.
LiPo BatteryChosen

Skips 3+ expensive ultra-small voltage regulators and their heat/current problems entirely. Runtime becomes the limiting factor, but an efficient design keeps that workable — a 3S 1,000mAh LiPo "stick" battery (~6 in long, fits the 1-inch ID) provides 12 Wh, giving 30+ minutes of active runtime, while its rectangular cross-section still leaves room for 4mm cables to pass through the hull (unlike a cylindrical Li-ion cell).

SYSTEMS / 01

Surface Tether Management

Spools

Simple and available — but any electrical cable on a spool needs a slip ring (~$20 for an 8-wire CAT6-rated low-power unit), and if PoE is used in any capacity, spools become a poor fit. Discrete tether floats also disrupt a clean coil, and the slip ring is a serious single point of failure.

Milk CrateChosen

The tether lies loose in a bin with a simple passive roller for smooth deployment. Discrete floats stop being a nuisance, and the system is cheap and reliable. It doesn't need motorizing — a second helper, or simply reeling in by hand once the mission is done and the pilot's hands are free, is enough.

SYSTEMS / 02

Construction Notes

Acetone Vapor Smoothing (ABS only)

A paper towel soaked in strong, pure acetone sits inside a chamber (metal, non-reactive plastic, or glass); the print is raised above it on a stand-off (like an upside-down bowl) so it isn't touching the towel directly, then covered. Acetone vapor melts the outer surface of the ABS print into a smooth, glossy finish; once removed, the acetone evaporates and the plastic rehardens.

Safety Acetone vapor is harmful to breathe and highly flammable — always do this in a well-ventilated space (ideally outside, while watching for dust settling on the softened surface). Check progress every few minutes: 10–20 min for small parts, 20–45 for medium, 45+ for larger. Stop once the layer lines vanish and the surface turns glossy — waiting past that point causes detail loss and warping.

3D Printing Notes

Infill and buoyancy: every 3D-printed part needs 100% infill, or its cavity filled with something solid like epoxy — otherwise trapped air or water randomly changes buoyancy underwater. Water through layer lines: the microscopic gaps between print layers are permeable; any print crossing a wet/dry boundary needs full epoxy coverage, or — for ABS parts — acetone vapor smoothing, to stay watertight.

SYSTEMS / 03

Areas of Improvement

Tether: the budget ruled out a high-quality tether from the start. The custom PVC/HDPE + float approach reaches a workable ~30 m depth ceiling, since pressure-related signal degradation and water ingress become real problems for non-gel-filled CAT6 beyond that. A deeper-rated tether would need gel-filling, higher-pressure-rated floats, and more length — all adding real cost.

Power (PoE): full PoE was the original ambition, but the voltage required to make it viable becomes expensive, dangerous, and time-consuming to implement safely at this scale. Given the limited budget and a priority on finishing for engineering competitions, the scope was narrowed to a battery-based system.
